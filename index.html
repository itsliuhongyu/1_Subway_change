Why can't I ride the Beijing Subway without a map anymore?

by Hongyu Liu


When I was in primary school, having too much free time, I used to love exploring Beijing, the city where I grew up.


I loved riding my foldable bicycle through the narrow downtown alleys. I loved dashing up the stairs of a double decker bus and sit on the front seat for the best view of the street. Also, I loved riding the subway which was warm in winter, and chill in summer.


By the time, there were only four subway lines in Beijing, and I rode every each of them. Again, I had too much free time.

Beijing Subway Line Map in 2005, from Baidu Baike


However, I got carried away as I went to middle school, high school, and eventually college. I stopped paying much attention to what is happening to the transportation system that I commute everyday.


Fast forward to 2020 when I returned home for summer, I realized that I can no longer navigate through this gigatic subway system without the help from my phone.


Where do these subway lines go to? When were they opened? More importantly, since when I start to loose track on the change in subway lines?

Beijing Subway Line Map in 2006, 2015, and 2023


To answer the question, I traced the opening year of each Beijing subway line with the exception of lines one and two. Both were already in use when I was born in 2000. The details for my calculations can be found in Github.


From 2000 to 2023, the Beijing subway expanded from 2 to 24 lines. The total line distance increased by seven folds, from 184.71km to 1446.34km. The boom in scale made it difficult for me to remember these new lines.


The Beijing subway system developed slowly before 2007. Line 13 was opened in 2002 initially as a commuter rail, and Batong Line was opened in 2003 as an extension of Line 1.


The opening of Line 5 in 2007 was a landmark for the city as it ran through several most populated neighborhoods in the city. I rode this line the most often when I was in primary school.


Line 5, as well as Line 10 and Line 8 (both partially opened in 2008), were built to prepare the city for the 2008 Olympic games. These lines were later expanded to be practical for long-term operations.


The total line distance increased the most in 2010. Due to the opening of several suburban lines, more than 200km of subway lines were added to the system.


The increase in length each year roughly shows a cycled pattern. It is because these lines were designed in phases. For example, the second phase was built from 2015-2021. Correspondingly, fewer subways were built in 2015 as the new plan was still in the blueprint stage. The next cycle will take place from 2022-2027.


In conclusion, I do not think there is a specific time that I suddenly lose my ability to navigate through the Beijing subway system. The system expanded rather steadily, but I was simply not paying attention to these changes. (I know it is not newsworthy at all. But this is what this assignment is about, right?)
